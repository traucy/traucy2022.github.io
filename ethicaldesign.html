Hi~! I’m Tracy, a product designer at Daybreak while studying design at the SFU’s School of Interactive Arts and Technology in Vancouver, Canada.
I excel in UI/visual design, interaction design, strategic thinking, prototyping, and illustration.

My ambition lies in understanding people, businesses, and non-humans to help create a holistic ecosystem of meaningful and purposeful interventions.
How people are moved by a product, how pixels are layed out, and the details that form each experience are what matters most to me.

During my free time, I study human anatomy, eat around Vancouver, and am doing my best to read this book about ways we could design for more than human-centered worlds.

This manifesto is the result of a directed studies course with 3 other fellow designers under the guidance of Ron Wakkary.

Be a facilitator

Have a critical lens and bring an outside perspective. Don’t impose your ideas, you are there to create a space of creative discovery.

Don’t Impose: Are you privileging your ideas? Or speaking over?

Reflect on your biases

Biases dictate the spaces you enter and who you are accountable to. Not all spaces are yours to design in, sometimes the best option is inaction.

Biases: What is your position in the world? Your privilege? Your lens?

Who: both human & non-human

Inaction: Are you the right design for this? Is this the right time?

Critical Use of Tools

Be critical of tools & methodologies, know their strengths and weaknesses. Most design tools were created by & for western designers & humans, so they may replicate marginalization.

Strengths and weaknesses: Who do they favour? Who do they disadvantage?

Respect Current Practices

Respect the current practices of communities - they are foundational & need to be understood. Uplift already working solutions, not all challenges need new designs.

Uplift already working solutions: Are you taking into consideration traditional, indigenous, and local knowledge?

Intersectional Ecosystem

Create an intersectional ecosystem that considers non-humans, history, & communities.

Intersectionality: Identity as race, gender, class, and sexual orientation and how they overlap

Everyone Designs

Design is decision making, not all design is called design. Communities define what it is and its use to overcome collective challenges.

Not all design is called design: Do non-human entities design?

Centre the Most Impacted

Those most affected should have the most say in the process. We can’t design for everyone, but ensure that those most marginalized are not left out of solutions.

Can’t design for everyone: Who are you leaving out and how are you doing it?

Multi-Lifespan Design

Design for multiple lifespans where designers are held accountable for the work they put into the world and how it evolves. Understand how you may be leaving behind or impacting non-humans.

Multiple-Lifespan: What will your design look like in 20 years? 50? 100?

Non-humans: Environment, Nature, Situations, Things, Robots, AI + Machine Learning

On a Smaller Scale

Small scale/situated projects that deeply impact a community should be prioritized over large-scale projects for the majority.

Impact: Value impact over intention

Design is Political

All design choices and actions are political, therefore reflection and constant critique is needed.

Political: What are your own values? Are there value tensions?

Ground in Research

Ground your decisions in research. Research is continual, ongoing, and should be a large part of your process.

Research: this can take shape in many forms, look outside of the traditional sense

Collaborate

Collectively & transparently design with the community. Aim to make decisions with, not for, where anyone can participate.

Collectively: Use participatory workshops, discussions and receive community feedback throughout the process

Transparently: Did you communicate your design values? How are you making your decisions?
